

3.7. Advancements in sensor technology for monitoring indoor environment
Indoor air pollution originates from household activities such as cooking, heating and cleaning as well as the release of VOCs from building materials, furniture and appliances. Understanding the extent of this pollution and its impacts is crucial for public health and policy formulation. Gas stoves, commonly utilized for culinary purposes in both residential and commercial settings, also draw significant attention. Research in this area involves the assessment of pollutant emissions including NO2, CO and PM2.5 from gas stoves. Sensor technology is essential for investigating indoor air quality (IAQ) in urban households. Prolonged exposure to elevated NO2 and CO levels is associated with an increased prevalence of respiratory symptoms particularly among children and elderly individuals. Recent advancements in sensor technology have led to a shift from traditional receptor models to continuous real-time monitoring methods including low-cost sensors (LCS). These sensors, due to their affordability and portability, are increasingly used for both personal and community-driven IAQ monitoring. However, their inconsistent calibration and validation across different researches have raised concerns about data reliability, highlighting the need for standardized methods to improve accuracy and long-term stability (Chojer et al., 2020). Recent studies suggest a hybrid approach combining traditional and continuous monitoring, to enhance the identification of pollution sources and to implement both preventive and reactive measures. This approach could potentially reshape IAQ management by providing more accurate source identification, thereby reducing health risks associated with indoor pollutants (Chojer et al., 2024).
For example, the “Home Health Boxes” (HHBs) to measure indoor air quality parameters (PM2.5, PM10, CO2, CO, NO2 and O3) using low-cost sensors (Tryner et al., 2021) as shown in Fig. 9. When collocated with reference monitors during a wildfire event in Colorado, HHBs provided data that closely matched reference monitors for gaseous pollutants. Improved calibration models reduced errors and improved data correlation, especially for O3. HHBs also provided accurate measurements for PM2.5 and PM10 with slight discrepancies from specific sensors. Hassani et al. (2023) examined the use of low-cost sensor systems (LCSs) deployed at residents' homes to enhance air quality monitoring in urban areas affected by residential wood burning. A network of 28 Airly LCSs was established in Kristiansand, Norway, over two winters. The sensor data correlated well with reference measurements (r = 0.86) for PM2.5 and high-resolution air quality maps were created using a data assimilation technique, significantly improving model accuracy. Moreover, the study examines indoor air pollution in charcoal-fueled kitchens in Uganda, focusing on PM2.5, heavy metals and CO (Nakora et al., 2020). The mean 24-hour PM2.5 concentration ranged from 0.449 to 0.526 mg/m3. Carbon monoxide levels were high at 41.52 ppm, exceeding WHO guidelines. Heavy metal concentrations were generally within safe levels but posed a chronic health risk. Kitchen size and ventilation had a significant impact on indoor air quality, highlighting the health risks associated with charcoal fuel use.

Despite these advancements, challenges remain, especially in real-world applications where low-cost sensors exhibit significant performance variability. The need for further refinement in sensor technology and calibration practices is emphasized to ensure these tools can reliably monitor IAQ on a broader scale (Sá et al., 2022). Moreover, integrating these advanced monitoring techniques into policy frameworks is essential to address the widespread and varied impacts of indoor air pollution effectively.
